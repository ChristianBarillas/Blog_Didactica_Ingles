1.Grammar translation

This method is based on the belief that learning a foreign language involves mastering its grammar rules and vocabulary, and that the best way to do this is by translating sentences and texts from the target language into the native language, and vice versa. In grammar-translation classes, students learn grammatical rules and then apply those rules by translating sentences between the target language and the native language. The method has two main goals: to enable students to read and translate literature written in the target language, and to further students’ general intellectual development.

At the beginning of the class we learned about the gramar tranlation method that was used in ancient times derived from the classical method of teaching Greek and Latin).

In this method, students are taught to analyze the grammatical structures of the target language and to memorize vocabulary through translation exercises. The teacher explains the rules of grammar and provides vocabulary lists, and the students are required to translate texts and sentences from the target language into their native language. This approach focuses on accuracy and often emphasizes reading and writing skills over speaking and listening.

2.Direct Method

In the Direct Method, teachers focus on teaching vocabulary and grammar through the use of pictures, objects, and gestures, rather than relying on translation. Students are encouraged to speak and listen to the target language from the beginning of their language learning journey, and are taught to associate meaning with the target language rather than with their native language.

And also it emphasizes oral communication and emphasizes the importance of learning a language in a natural way, similar to how native speakers learn their first language.

Unlike the GTM, the natural method discards any use of the mother tongue in the classroom and emphasizes the learning of the second language to be more like the first one that was learned, the grammar is taught inductively, meaning it is taught in context and when a student makes a mistake it is not the end of the world, this method does not focus on the error correction.

3.Audiolingual method

The Audio-Lingual method of teaching had its origins during World War I when it became known as the Army Method. The armies needed to become orally proficient in the languages of their allies and enemies as quickly as possible. This was first method to be based on linguistic theory and behavioral psychology. This suggests that students be taught phonology, morphology, and syntax of the language

We also learned in class that there are exercises that are used to teach structural patterns.

This method emphasizes the teaching of listening and speaking before reading and writing. It uses dialogues as the main form of language presentation and drills as the main training techniques.

Its basic principles are:
• Separation of language skills into listening, speaking, reading and writing.
• Use of dialogues as the chief means of presenting the language.
• Emphasis on certain practice techniques: mimicry, memorization and pattern drills.
• Discouraging the use of the mother tongue in the classroom.

Based on Skinner's Behaviorism theory, it assumed that a human being can be trained using a system of reinforcement. Correct behavior receives positive feedback, while errors receive negative feedback.

4.Silent method

The Silent Way was founded in the early 1970s by the Egyptian mathematician and educator Caleb Gattegno. It is based on the idea that language learning can be enhanced in three main ways: discovery rather than teaching; problem-solving in the target language; the use of physical tools.

Multiple Intelligences

Hello guy! in the class we're learning about this wonderful topic and let me ask you something have you ever heard of multiple intelligences? It's an exciting idea that says there isn't just one type of intelligence, but many. Each one of us has different and special skills that make us smart in different ways. For example, some people are really good with words and languages. They might love reading books, telling stories, or learning new languages. This is called linguistic intelligence. Others are great at solving problems and figuring out puzzles. They might love math or science, and enjoy experimenting and discovering new things. This is called logical-mathematical intelligence. Some people have a talent for art and design. They might love to draw, paint, or create things with their hands. This is called spatial intelligence. Others are fantastic at sports and physical activities. They might love to dance, play soccer, or climb trees. This is called bodily-kinesthetic intelligence. Some people are really good at music. They might love to sing, play an instrument, or compose their own songs. This is called musical intelligence. There are also people who are great at understanding and connecting with other people. They might be good listeners, great friends, or natural leaders. This is called interpersonal intelligence. Some people are really good at understanding their own feelings and thoughts. They might be good at reflecting on their experiences, setting goals, and making plans. This is called intrapersonal intelligence. Finally, there are people who are great at understanding and appreciating the natural world. They might love animals, plants, or rocks, and enjoy exploring and observing the world around them. This is called naturalistic intelligence.


1.Linguistic intelligence: the ability to use language effectively, including reading, writing, speaking, and listening.
2.Logical-mathematical intelligence: the ability to reason, problem-solve, and think abstractly.
3.Spatial intelligence: the ability to think in three dimensions, visualize objects, and mentally manipulate them.
4.Bodily-kinesthetic intelligence: the ability to control and coordinate physical movement and handle objects skillfully.
5.Musical intelligence: the ability to recognize, create, and appreciate music and musical patterns.
6.Interpersonal intelligence: the ability to understand and relate to other people, including recognizing their motivations and emotions.
7.Intrapersonal intelligence: the ability to understand one's own thoughts, feelings, and motivations.
8.Naturalistic intelligence: the ability to observe and appreciate nature, including recognizing and categorizing plants, animals, and natural phenomena.
